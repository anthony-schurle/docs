**PSET 1:**
Let $X = \{(x,y)\in \mathbb{R}^2 : x^2 + y^2 \le 1\}$ and define an equivalence relation on X as follows $(x,y)R(x',y')$ if they both have length 1. Prove that $\mathbb{C}P^1 := \mathbb{C}^2 mod(C* := \mathbb{C} \backslash \{0\})$ as defined in class is homeomorphic to $X / R$, with the quotient topology. What familiar space are they both homeomorphic to (no proof needed)?
**Solution:**
To prove $X/R \cong \mathbb{C}P^1$, we identify $X$ with the unit disk $\mathbb{D}$ and define $\tilde{f}: \mathbb{D} \to \mathbb{C}P^1$ by $\tilde{f}(z) = [2z : 1-|z|^2]$. This map is continuous and surjective. On the boundary $\partial\mathbb{D}$, where $|z|=1$, we have $\tilde{f}(z) = [2z : 0]$. Since $[w_1 : 0] = [1 : 0]$ for all $w_1 \in \mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}$, the entire boundary maps to a single point in $\mathbb{C}P^1$, satisfying the equivalence relation $R$. On the interior $|z|<1$, the map is injective: if $[2z : 1-|z|^2] = [2z' : 1-|z'|^2]$, the non-zero real second coordinates force the scaling factor $\lambda$ to be $1$, implying $z=z'$. By Corollary 22.3, $\tilde{f}$ induces a continuous bijection $f: \mathbb{D}/R \to \mathbb{C}P^1$. To show $f$ is a homeomorphism, we examine the neighborhood of the boundary point $P = [1:0]$. A neighborhood $U_\epsilon$ in $\mathbb{C}P^1$ consists of points $[w_1:w_2]$ where $|w_2/w_1| < \epsilon$. The pullback $\tilde{f}^{-1}(U_\epsilon)$ satisfies $|(1-|z|^2)/2z| < \epsilon$. For $|z|$ near $1$, this inequality defines an open annulus $r < |z| \le 1$, which is an open neighborhood of the boundary in the quotient topology. Combined with the fact that $\tilde{f}$ restricted to the interior is a homeomorpWhat grahism onto $\mathbb{C}P^1 \setminus \{P\}$ (matching the standard complex chart), $\tilde{f}$ is a quotient map. Thus, $X/R \cong \mathbb{C}P^1$, and both are homeomorphic to the 2-sphere $S^2$**.

**Problem 1:**
Let $\Gamma$ and $\Gamma '$ be two topologies on X. If $\Gamma \subseteq \Gamma '$, what does connectedness of X in one topology imply about connectedness in the other?
**Solution:**
We show that connectedness in $\Gamma '$ implies connectedness in $\Gamma$. Suppose X is not connected in $\Gamma$, such there there are two disjoint, open sets U and V where $X = U \cup V$. Since $\Gamma \subseteq \Gamma '$, U and V are also disjoint, open sets of $\Gamma '$ whose union is X. By definition, X is disconnected under $\Gamma '$. We now show that connectedness in $\Gamma$ does not imply connectedness in $\Gamma'$ with a counterexample. Let $X = \{a,b\}$ and define $\Gamma$ to be the indiscrete topology while $\Gamma '$ is the discrete topology. $\Gamma$ is connected by construction and X is separated by $\{a\}, \{b\}$ in $\Gamma '$ so that $\Gamma '$ is not connected. $\blacksquare$

**Problem 2:**
Let $\{A_n\}$ be a sequence of connected subspaces of X, such that $A_n \cap A_{n+1} \ne \emptyset$ for all n. Show that $\bigcup A_n$ is connected.
**Solution:**
We show by induction for the finite case that $U_k = \bigcup_{n=1}^k A_n$ is connected for all k. $U_1 = A_1$ is trivially connected. Now suppose $U_k$ is connected and consider $U_{k+1} = U_k \cup A_{k+1}$. By assumption, $A_k \cap A_{k+1} \ne \emptyset$ and since $A_k \subseteq U_k$, it follows that $U_k \cap A_{k+1} \ne \emptyset$. By Theorem 23.3, $U_{k+1}$ must be connected. Therefore, $U_k$ is connected for all k. Notice that $A_1 \subseteq U_1 \subseteq U_2 \subseteq ...$ and so all $U_k$ share a point in common because $A_1$ is nonempty ($A_1 \cap A_2 \ne \emptyset$) and contained in each $U_k$. Thus, by theorem 23.3, the union of all $U_k$ for all k is connected. $\blacksquare$
