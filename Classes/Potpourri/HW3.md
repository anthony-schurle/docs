1) Find the error in the following "simple" proof of Menger's theorem (3.3.1). Let X be an $A-B$ separator of minimum size. Denote by $G_A$ the subgraph of G induced by X and all components of $G-X$ that contain at least one vertex of A, and define $G_B$ correspondingly. By the minimality of X, there can be no $A-X$ separator in $G_A$ with fewer than $\#X$ vertices, so $G_A$ contains k disjoint A-X paths by induction. Similarly, $G_B$ contains k disjoint X - B paths, and together, all these paths form the desired $A-B$ paths in G.

The error is in the concatenation step. The inductive hypothesis guarantees $k$ disjoint paths from $A$ to $X$ in $G_A$ and $k$ disjoint paths from $X$ to $B$ in $G_B$. However, it does not guarantee these two sets of paths share the same endpoints in $X$. The inductive applications act independently on $G_A$ and $G_B$. If $|X| > k$, the paths in $G_A$ might select a different subset of $X$ than the paths in $G_B$. Even if $|X| = k$, the mapping of paths arriving from $A$ might not align with the paths departing for $B$. There is no logic shown to ensure they can be "connected" into $k$ continuous, vertex-disjoint $A-B$ paths. $\blacksquare$

2) Let $k\ge2$. Show that in a k-connected graph of order at least 2k, any k vertices lie on a common cycle.

Let $S$ be a set of $k$ vertices in a $k$-connected graph $G$. Let $C$ be a cycle in $G$ that contains the maximum possible number of vertices from $S$. Choose $C$ to maximize its length $|V(C)|$. If $V(C)$ contains all of $S$, we are done. Suppose there exists some $v \in S$ such that $v \notin V(C)$. We first show $|V(C)| \ge k$. Suppose $|V(C)| < k$. Since $G$ is $k$-connected, the removal of fewer than $k$ vertices cannot disconnect the graph. Thus, $G - V(C)$ is a single connected component containing at least $2k - (k-1) = k+1$ vertices. Since $\delta(G) \ge k$ and $|V(C)| < k$, every vertex in $C$ has at least one neighbor in $G - V(C)$. For any edge $xy \in E(C)$, $x$ and $y$ both have neighbors in $G - V(C)$. Since $G - V(C)$ is connected, there is a path from $x$ to $y$ through $G - V(C)$. Replacing the edge $xy$ in $C$ with this path creates a longer cycle containing the same vertices of $S$. This contradicts the maximal length of $C$. Therefore, $|V(C)| \ge k$. Since $G$ is $k$-connected and $v \notin V(C)$, we can apply the Fan Lemma to the vertex $v$ and the set $V(C)$ in $G$. This yields $k$ internally disjoint paths from $v$ to $V(C)$. These paths share only $v$ and arrive at exactly $k$ distinct terminal vertices on $C$. These endpoints partition $C$ into $k$ segments. Since $v \notin V(C)$, $C$ contains at most $k-1$ vertices of $S$. By the Pigeonhole Principle, at least one segment between two path endpoints contains no vertices of $S$. Replacing this segment with the two paths through $v$ yields a new cycle $C'$ containing $v$ and all vertices of $S$ originally on $C$. Thus, $C'$ contains more vertices of $S$ than $C$, contradicting the maximality of $C$. Therefore, any $k$ vertices lie on a common cycle. $\blacksquare$

3) 
a) A fullerene is a molecule that is made up entirely of carbon atoms forming a cubic plane graph all whose regions (this includes the outer region!) are pentagons or hexagons. Show that, since carbon atoms can form double bonds, every such graph can be realized in principle by (4-valent) carbon atoms.

To show the graph can be realized by 4-valent carbon atoms, it is sufficient to prove that a fullerene graph contains a 1-factor. A fullerene is a cubic (3-regular) plane graph consisting only of pentagonal and hexagonal faces. Since it consists entirely of bounded faces, it is bridgeless (and 3-connected). By Petersen's Theorem, every cubic, bridgeless graph contains a 1-factor. Assigning double bonds to the edges in this 1-factor and single bonds to the remaining edges (which form a 2-factor) satisfies the 4-valency of the carbon atoms. $\blacksquare$

b) Fullerenes are less stable if they contain adjacent pentagons. Show that stable fullerenes have at least 60 carbon atoms.

Let $v, e,$ and $f$ be the number of vertices, edges, and faces, respectively. By Euler's formula, $v - e + f = 2$. Since the graph is cubic, $3v = 2e$. Let $f_5$ and $f_6$ be the number of pentagonal and hexagonal faces, so $f = f_5 + f_6$. Summing the edges of each face gives $5f_5 + 6f_6 = 2e = 3v$. Substituting these into Euler's formula gives $v - \frac{3v}{2} + f_5 + f_6 = 2$, and so $-\frac{v}{2} + f_5 + f_6 = 2$. Multiplying by 6 gives $-3v + 6f_5 + 6f_6 = 12$. Substituting $3v = 5f_5 + 6f_6$ yields $-(5f_5 + 6f_6) + 6f_5 + 6f_6 = 12$, so $f_5 = 12$. Every fullerene has exactly 12 pentagons. A stable fullerene has no two pentagons sharing an edge. In a cubic plane graph, three edges meet at every vertex, so three faces meet at every vertex. The dual graph of a cubic plane graph is a triangulation. A vertex in the primal graph corresponds to a triangular face in the dual. The three faces meeting at a primal vertex correspond to three mutually adjacent vertices forming a $K_3$ in the dual. Since every pair of vertices in a $K_3$ is connected by an edge, every pair of faces meeting at the primal vertex shares an edge.  If two pentagons shared a vertex, they would share an edge, contradicting stability. Thus, the 12 pentagons are vertex-disjoint. Since each pentagon has 5 vertices, the graph has at least $12 \times 5 = 60$ vertices. $\blacksquare$

4) Prove Proposition 1.7.3(ii), that is, show that if $\Delta(X)\le3$, then every IX contains a TX; thus, every minor with maximum degree at most 3 of a graph is also its topological minor.

Let $Y$ be an $IX$. There is a set of disjoint connected subgraphs $\{V_x : x \in V(X)\}$ in $G$ such that for every edge $xy \in E(X)$, there is an edge in $G$ between $V_x$ and $V_y$. Since $\Delta(X) \le 3$, each branch set $V_x$ connects to at most 3 other branch sets. Let the boundary edges leaving $V_x$ be $e_1, e_2, e_3$, and let their respective endpoints within $V_x$ be $v_1, v_2, v_3$. Since $V_x$ is connected, there is a minimal tree $T \subseteq V_x$ spanning these boundary vertices. If there are 1 or 2 boundary vertices (or they coincide), $T$ is a vertex or a path, and we let $u_x$ be any vertex in $T$. If there are 3 boundary vertices and $T$ is a path, let $u_x$ be an internal vertex. If $T$ branches, it contains exactly one vertex of degree 3; let this be $u_x$. So, $T$ provides a central vertex $u_x \in V_x$ and internally disjoint paths within $V_x$ from $u_x$ to each $v_i$. Map each $x \in V(X)$ to $u_x \in V(G)$. The edges of $TX$ are formed by the path in $V_x$ to the boundary edge, the boundary edge, and the path in $V_y$ to $u_y$. These paths are constructed within disjoint sets $V_x$, so they are internally disjoint. $\blacksquare$

5) Determine all complete multipartite graphs $K_{r_1,...,r_n}$ that are planar. In other words, find conditions on the $r_i$ and n that result in planarity.

Let $r_1 \ge r_2 \ge \dots \ge r_n \ge 1$. We analyze the number of partitions, $n$. Case $n = 1$: $K_{r_1}$ is an independent set of $r_1$ vertices, which is planar for all $r_1$. Case $n = 2$: $K_{r_1, r_2}$ is a complete bipartite graph. By Kuratowski's Theorem, it contains the non-planar $K_{3,3}$ if $r_1 \ge 3$ and $r_2 \ge 3$. Thus, it is planar if and only if $\min(r_1, r_2) \le 2$. Case $n = 3$: Grouping the second and third partitions yields a $K_{r_1, r_2+r_3}$ subgraph. If $r_1 \ge 3$, any configuration with $r_2+r_3 \ge 3$ contains $K_{3,3}$. To maintain planarity, we need $r_2 + r_3 \le 2$. Since $r_i \ge 1$, this requires $r_2 = 1$ and $r_3 = 1$. The graph $K_{r_1, 1, 1}$ is planar. If $r_1 \le 2$, the maximal graph is $K_{2,2,2}$, the skeleton of an octahedron, which is planar. Since planarity is inherited by subgraphs, subgraphs like $K_{2,2,1}$ are also planar. Case $n = 4$: If $r_1 \ge 3$, grouping the rest gives a $K_{r_1, r_2+r_3+r_4}$ subgraph where $r_2+r_3+r_4 \ge 3$, creating a $K_{3,3}$ minor, so it is non-planar. If $r_1 \le 2$, $K_{2,1,1,1}$ is $K_5$ minus an edge, which is planar. However, $K_{2,2,1,1}$ has $V=6$ and $E=13$. This violates the planar edge bound $E \le 3V - 6$, so it is non-planar. Case $n \ge 5$: The smallest such graph is $K_{1,1,1,1,1}$, which is $K_5$. By Kuratowski's Theorem, this is non-planar. No complete multipartite graphs for $n \ge 5$ are planar. $\blacksquare$

6) For every $n>1$, find a bipartite graph on 2n vertices, ordered in such a way that the greedy algorithm uses n rather than 2 colors.

Let $G$ be a bipartite graph with partitions $A = \{a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n\}$ and $B = \{b_1, b_2, \dots, b_n\}$. Define the edge set as $E(G) = \{\{a_i, b_j\} : i \ne j\}$. This is a complete bipartite graph $K_{n,n}$ missing a perfect matching. Order the $2n$ vertices as the alternating sequence $a_1, b_1, a_2, b_2, \dots, a_n, b_n$. For the first pair, $a_1$ is assigned color 1. Since $b_1$ is not adjacent to $a_1$, $b_1$ also receives color 1. For the second pair, $a_2$ is adjacent to $b_1$, so it is assigned color 2. Since $b_2$ is adjacent to $a_1$ and not $a_2$, it receives color 2. Inductively, consider the pair $a_k$ and $b_k$. When the algorithm processes $a_k$, it is adjacent to $b_1, b_2, \dots, b_{k-1}$. These vertices have been assigned colors $1, 2, \dots, k-1$, so $a_k$ takes color $k$. Next, $b_k$ is adjacent to $a_1, a_2, \dots, a_{k-1}$ but not to $a_k$. Thus, $b_k$ also takes color $k$. This pattern continues up to $a_n, b_n$. The greedy algorithm uses $n$ colors, even though $G$ is 2-colorable. $\blacksquare$
